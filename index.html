1

Fake Bank Alert & Reversal Scams

The Story

Scammers send fake SMS credit alerts that look identical to real bank notifications — or show a manipulated screenshot. No money actually moves. The reversal variant is worse: you “refund” real money, then the original fraudulent credit disappears.

Good intentions don’t pay your bills. Never send money back based on an alert — check your actual account balance first. If the money hasn’t settled, the scam has.

Reveal · Red flags

Thread B was the fake

  • No real SMS history Thread A has older alerts. Thread B is a lone message pretending to be GTBank
  • The sender is a phone number Real GTBank alerts come from the bank sender ID — not “via +234…” under the GTBank label
  • The body is designed to look right Scammers copy the Acct / Amt / Desc / Bal layout on purpose — body alone is not proof
  • No matching transaction in the app Open GTBank / your wallet — if the credit isn’t there, the SMS is theatre
  • Urgency + screenshot + distraction Fuel, POS, “refund me now” — they rush you so you don’t confirm the balance

The reversal trap · Extremely common

You “refund” real money. Then theirs vanishes.

  1. Fraudulent credit — money hits your account via a compromised third-party wallet
  2. Urgent call — “Bros, I sent money by mistake. Please send it back.”
  3. You refund — you send your own real money back
  4. Bank reverses — hours later the original transfer is reversed as fraud
  5. You’re left holding the loss — their money is gone; yours is gone too

Also common: a fuel buyer shows a transfer screenshot, chats to distract you, or rushes you — then the “credit” never settles or gets reversed.

How to spot fake SMS / screenshot alerts

Habits that stop the loss

  • 1 · Check the sender Bank name sender ID — or just a phone number wearing a bank label?
  • 2 · Compare with past alerts If you keep old alerts, does this one match how your bank usually writes?
  • 3 · Beware “give me your details” Buyer asking for phone number and account number may be priming a fake alert.
  • 4 · Confirm in the app OPay / Moniepoint / PalmPay — confirm every screenshot inside the app before you release anything.
  • 5 · Watch for reversals Some apps show reversed credits — check before handing over cash or goods.
  • 6 · Use every channel No SMS? Check email. Then check the live balance yourself.

Live action: never release cash, fuel, or goods until your balance has actually changed.

2

WhatsApp Account Hijack

The Story

A message lands — sometimes from a hacked contact, sometimes an unknown +234 number. It sounds harmless. Then they ask for a “verification code.” That code is your WhatsApp login. Share it, and your account is theirs within seconds.

Your WhatsApp verification code is the key to your digital life. Anyone who asks for it is trying to steal your identity. Enable two-step verification right now.

The trap · Verification code

They want your WhatsApp login code

If a code arrives that you didn’t request, someone is already trying to log in as you. Anyone who then asks for it is the attacker.

What happens next

Within 60 seconds, you’re locked out

  • They message your contacts for money “I need urgent help, send ₦50,000” — using your name and profile photo
  • They join your family & work groups Scam links and “help me” messages hit everyone who trusts you
  • They harvest chats for blackmail Private conversations, voice notes, and photos become leverage
  • They clone your voice with AI Voice notes from your chats are enough to call relatives as “you”

Press Next / Space to reveal each impact, then continue.

How to spot it · Norton

WhatsApp scam red flags

  • Unsolicited contact Unexpected texts from unknown numbers — especially “business” or “support” impersonators
  • Urgency / panic Messages that rush you so you don’t think — emergencies, expiring codes, “send now”
  • Money or personal info Requests for cash, bank details, passwords, or verification codes over chat
  • Generic greetings “Hi friend”, “Hello dear” — or “is this you?” — when they should already know
  • Suspicious links / files Unexpected links or downloads can lead to phishing sites or malware
  • Display name ≠ identity A stolen photo and saved name are not proof — verify on another channel

Same patterns show up across fake jobs, romance, investment, and “WhatsApp Gold” scams too.

How to stop it

Habits that protect the account

  • 1 · Never share the code WhatsApp already says “Don’t share this code.” Anyone asking for it is a thief — even if the chat looks like family.
  • 2 · Turn on two-step verification Settings → Account → Two-step verification → set a PIN. Even with the SMS code, they can’t register without your PIN.
  • 3 · Tighten privacy settings Limit who can see your photo / last seen / status, and who can add you to groups — stolen profiles fuel impersonation.
  • 4 · Verify before you trust Strange tone or money request from a “contact”? Call them on another line. Ignore random money requests.
  • 5 · If you’re hit — move fast Block & report the sender, re-register your number, enable 2-step, then warn contacts: ignore money requests from “you.”
  • 6 · Live action right now Open WhatsApp → Settings → Account → Two-step verification → turn it on before we leave this slide.

Audience challenge: everyone enable two-step verification before the next story.

4

Phishing — Fake Bank SMS & Email

The Story

An urgent SMS or email looks like your bank, NIMC, or a courier. The logo feels right — but the link leads to a fake page built to harvest your NIN, BVN, passwords, and bank details.

Government agencies never rush you toward a link via SMS. Urgency plus a link means predator, not regulator. Always check official .gov.ng domains — and open your bank app yourself.

Reveal · Red flags

Same fear. Same trap.

  • Artificial deadlines “15 mins,” “2 hours,” “10 minutes,” “6PM today,” “30 minutes,” “1 hour” — panic timers that don’t exist at real banks
  • Threat language REVERSED, DISCONNECTED, DEACTIVATED, DESTROYED, permanently blocked, frozen, blacklisting
  • Lookalike domains gtbank-verify.com / .net, nin-sim-verify.com, firstbank-card.com, dhl-ngr.com, cbn-verify.org — not official sites
  • Suspicious sender Phone numbers wearing bank names, or email from @gtbank-verify.net instead of the real bank domain
  • They want a click (then your secrets) Confirm / re-link / reactivate / pay / re-validate — always via a link that harvests BVN, PIN, password, OTP

Common Nigerian examples

Same script. Different costumes.

Scam type Message Red flags
NIN-SIM Linkage “Your NIN-SIM linkage has expired. Click here to re-link: nin-sim-verify.com” Official gov’t never sends links; urgency
Bank verification “Your GTBank account has been restricted. Update your details here: gtbank-verify.net” Fake domain (.net not .com); urgency
Delivery scam “Your DHL package is held at customs. Pay ₦3,500 to release: dhl-ngr.com” Fake domain; courier doesn’t charge via SMS link
ATM card upgrade “Your ATM card has been deactivated. Click here to reactivate: firstbank-card.com” Banks never deactivate cards via SMS

Pattern: fear + deadline + link. Always open the official app or type the known website yourself.

Once they have BVN + PIN/OTP, they can open accounts, take loans, and drain wallets in your name.

How to stop it

Habits that kill phishing

  • 1 · Don’t tap SMS links Open your bank or wallet app yourself. Never “verify,” “reactivate,” or “confirm receipt” from a text.
  • 2 · Read the real domain gtbank.com ≠ gtbank-verify.com / .net. Government is .gov.ng — not random .com sites.
  • 3 · Guard BVN, NIN, PIN, OTP No bank, NIMC agent, or courier needs you to paste those into a link from SMS.
  • 4 · Urgency is the weapon “Restricted,” “expired,” “held at customs” — pause. Call the official number on the back of your card / known site.
  • 5 · Check sender history Compare with older bank alerts. A random +234 wearing a bank name is a tell.
  • 6 · Live action Delete any “verify your account” SMS sitting in your inbox — and never reuse a password that appeared on a fake page.

Rule of thumb: if it arrives as a link asking for secrets, close it and go through the official app.

Loan App Harassment & Data Blackmail

The Story

A quick loan app (WeCredit, Hen Credit, Cash Door — blacklisted by FCCPC) asks for Contacts, SMS, Call Logs, Photos, and Microphone. You accept because you need the money. Miss a payment — and they bomb your mother, pastor, and boss.

A loan app doesn’t need your contact list or photo gallery. Revoke unnecessary permissions today, or someone else decides who sees your private information.

What they do with the harvest · Real samples

This is what the messages look like

  • Urgency + caps lock “TODAY”, “NOW!!!”, “TIL 12NOON” — panic so you don’t think
  • Reputation blackmail “Unfixable damage… reputation and integrity” — shame as a weapon
  • Contact-list bombing Threat to blast your photo & IDs to contacts, social media, even a “municipality FB page”
  • Proof the permissions were abused They can only threaten your contacts because the app already harvested them

Numbers redacted. Debt recovery turned into data blackmail.

Key facts

Enforcement is catching up — slowly

45Loan apps blacklisted by FCCPC in January 2026 alone — including names like WeCredit, Hen Credit, Cash Door
500+Digital lenders registered — many more still operate in the grey zone
NDPCYou can sue for data privacy violations when apps abuse contacts and photos

Report abuse: lenderstaskforce@fccpc.gov.ng — Federal Competition & Consumer Protection Commission lenders task force.

Blacklist status matters: if an app needs your entire contact list to lend ₦20,000, walk away.

Live action · Do this now

Revoke. Report. Refuse the next “Allow.”

  • 1 · Open Permission Manager Android: Settings → Apps → Permission manager → Contacts. iPhone: Settings → Privacy & Security → Contacts.
  • 2 · Ask one question Does this app need my contacts to work? If no — tap Deny / Off.
  • 3 · Strip loan-style apps Contacts, SMS, Call logs, Photos, Microphone — revoke what isn’t essential. Uninstall blacklisted apps.
  • 4 · Report harassment Email evidence to lenderstaskforce@fccpc.gov.ng. Document screenshots of messages to your contacts.
  • 5 · Know your rights NDPC data privacy rules apply. Weaponising your contacts is not “normal recovery.”
  • 6 · Before the next install If permissions look like a fishing net, the loan interest is not the only cost.

Audience challenge: revoke at least one unnecessary Contacts permission before we move on.

Cryptocurrency & Forex & Investment Ponzi Schemes

The Story

CBEX promised 100% returns in 30 days. Nigerians poured in an estimated ₦1.3 trillion before it collapsed. Different names — crypto “signals,” forex “mentors,” MMM, Racksterli, XM Future Music — same script. People don’t just lose money; they hand over BVN, IDs, selfies, and bank details to anonymous admins before the platform vanishes.

No legitimate investment guarantees fixed returns. If it sounds too good to be true, it’s a Ponzi scheme wearing a new name — and your cash and personal data are both on the table.

Major Nigerian Ponzi collapses

Different names. Same ending.

Scheme Losses Promise
CBEX / Crypto Bridge ~₦1.3 trillion (media-reported) 100% returns in 30 days
Racksterli (Black Gold) ~₦1 billion Fast crypto doubling
XM Future Music Group Ongoing 100% in 30 days for “listening to songs”
Mavrodi / MMM-style Recurring Help others = get paid

SEC Nigeria warned CBEX was unregistered. Naming changes. The math never does.

Common pattern

How the machine actually works

  • 1 · Fake room Telegram / WhatsApp group packed with “testimonies” and screenshot “alerts”
  • 2 · Fake proof “I invested ₦50,000 and got ₦150,000 in 2 weeks” — theatre to create FOMO
  • 3 · Classic Ponzi Early members are paid with new investors’ money — not with real profits
  • 4 · The crash Platform “goes down,” withdrawals freeze, admin disappears — your money is gone

If a return depends on recruiting the next person, you’re not investing — you’re the exit liquidity.

How to stop it

Rules that kill Ponzi bait

  • 1 · Guaranteed = scam Leave any chat that promises fixed high returns on a schedule. Real markets don’t work that way.
  • 2 · Check SEC Search the operator on the Securities & Exchange Commission Nigeria lists before you send money.
  • 3 · No WhatsApp “investments” Regulated platforms don’t run deposit addresses through anonymous group admins.
  • 4 · Ignore the alert brag Screenshot testimonies prove nothing. Early payouts are bait — not proof of a business.
  • 5 · Warn your circle When a cousin forwards “join my VIP group,” reply with this story — not with money.

If it sounds too good to be true, it already is.
